Josh Bailey · Silverstone

Pressure-tested delivery. People-first support.

I'm an IT Desktop Analyst at Aston Martin F1, supporting HR, Finance, and Commercial — the parts of a season nobody puts on television. Before this I ran the helpdesk for Manshead Academy on the MSP side, was on the IT deployment team at Red Bull Racing, and spent five years on the Genius Bar at Apple. Different rooms, the same shape of problem. The throughline is endpoint work done well, and people who leave the conversation in a better mood than they started it.

02 Who am I

"Great. Tell me more about Josh."

I'm at my best when the work is invisible and the people who depend on it are getting on with theirs. I'd rather ship the boring tool that solves the problem than the clever one that announces it. Most of what I think about — at work and off — is in how things actually run, versus how the diagram says they run.

Outside the day job I play metal guitar, lean toward the Periphery end of the dial more than I should admit, and read more about infrastructure-as-code than is strictly healthy. I'm not particularly online.

A few things that are true
  • I take "boring" as a compliment about engineering choices.
  • I write the documentation first when I'm sure of what I'm doing, and last when I'm not.
  • I'd rather a small tool, used daily, than a platform that needs a launch plan.
  • I tend to work in Ctrl+K apps and a single window with a lot of tabs I refuse to close.
  • I will out-stubborn anyone in a meeting on whether the script should run as SYSTEM or as the user.
03 What am I like

"But what is Josh like to work with? "

The fewer people who notice the IT, the better the IT was.

I came up through helpdesks and benches, and I've never quite shaken the conviction that good IT is a craft — closer to a stage crew than a software team. The reward is the show going on, not the credit for it.

  1. i.
    Race weekends.

    Twelve a year. A fixed deadline that doesn't move because the meeting ran long. Either the laptop works at 13:00 in Suzuka or it doesn't.

  2. ii.
    Small tools, used daily.

    The script that saves the on-call engineer twenty minutes at 2 a.m. is worth more than the platform that gets a launch post.

  3. iii.
    A guitar in the other room.

    Metal, mostly — the technical kind. Practising clean tone teaches the same thing as practising clean code: do less, on purpose.

  4. iv.
    Writing it down.

    Half of why this site exists. The other half is so the next Josh — at the next team — doesn't have to learn the same lessons in the same order.
